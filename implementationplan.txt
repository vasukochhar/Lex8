Part 0 — One-Page Quick Read
What we are building
Lex8 is a collaborative AI workspace for lawyers — the same product surface category as Legora and
Harvey (Assistant, Tabular Review, Workflows, Word add-in, research, drafting, collaboration). The
difference is what sits underneath every action: Anchor8, our governance fabric, which intercepts every
action by every agent before it executes and stamps a cryptographically signed defensibility certificate
on the output.
Why it wins
• The wedge: no other legal AI platform can prove, to a regulator or a malpractice insurer, that a
specific output was free of hallucination, privilege leak, bias, or unauthorized action. Lex8 can —
by construction.
• The timing: EU AI Act full enforcement is live (Aug 2, 2026). Article 12, 13, and 14 require
traceable audit logs, transparency, and effective human oversight for high-risk AI systems. Legal
AI is high-risk. Nobody else ships the forensic narrative the law actually requires.
• The scope: every Lex8 customer is an Anchor8 customer. Lex8 is the showcase product that
makes Anchor8's all-archetype governance pitch tangible. The two-product GTM compounds.
How the 8 archetypes show up
Every meaningful piece of legal work touches all eight agent types. Lex8 maps each to a named product
module — see Part 3 — so the demo has a natural reason to invoke each archetype in sequence on a
single case.
Demo arc (~7 minutes, 3 acts)
1. Act I — A complex M&A dispute flows through all 8 modules. The platform delivers a polished
motion, settlement memo, and filed docket entry. Each artifact carries a green Anchor8 Verified
chip.
2. Act II — Three live intercepts: a hallucinated citation (Lane 2 kill, ~50ms), a privileged-paragraph
leak via e-filing (Lane 3 Tribunal — two jurors vote BLOCK independently, sent to Lane 4 human
review), and a demographically biased jury-strike recommendation (constitutional constraint hit,
full TreeSHAP attribution shown).
3. Act III — The Defensibility Console. Every action signed to an agent DID. Click any output and
download an EU AI Act Article 14-compliant forensic narrative as PDF.
Build commitment
Three weeks end-to-end via Antigravity. Week 1: scaffolding, identity, the eight module skeletons. Week
2: feature depth, Anchor8 wiring, the three demo intercepts. Week 3: polish, dry runs, fallbacks.
Detailed schedule in Part 8.

Part 1 — Product Vision & Positioning
1.1 The problem we solve
Every law firm with a partner-level signature on the line currently faces an unsolved problem: AI tools
produce great work, but no tool produces work the firm can confidently file in court without manual
verification of every citation, every fact, every privileged passage, and every potential bias risk.
The Mata v. Avianca incident (Schwartz, S.D.N.Y. 2023) made the cost of an unverified AI output visible:
$5,000 in sanctions, career-ending publicity, and a precedent now cited in dozens of subsequent
malpractice referrals. Every managing partner has internalized this. None of them have a defensible
answer when their associates use AI.
EU AI Act Article 14 (full enforcement 2 Aug 2026) makes the legal exposure structural rather than
incidental. Legal AI is classified as a high-risk system. Human oversight must be "effective." Audit logs
must be "traceable." Forensic narratives must be reconstructable on demand. Today, no legal AI
platform on the market satisfies this standard — they have great product but no governance scaffold.
Lex8 is the first legal AI platform where the answer to "can you prove this output is defensible?" is a
cryptographically signed yes — generated automatically, on every action, by Anchor8.
1.2 The vision
Lex8 is a collaborative AI operating system for legal work — drafting, research, tabular review, workflow
automation, client portal — built on top of Anchor8's all-archetype governance fabric. The product
surface is competitive with Legora and Harvey; the moat is in the governance layer no competitor has.
Every action a Lex8 agent takes is intercepted by Anchor8 before execution: schema-validated in Lane 1,
scored by the 12-detector Adaptive Engine in Lane 2 (~200ms), escalated to the dual-juror Tribunal in
Lane 3 if the score lands in the uncertainty band (~3.5s), and routed to attorney review in Lane 4 for
irreversible high-stakes calls. Every verdict produces an EU AI Act Article 14-compliant forensic narrative,
signed with AWS KMS keys held by the firm's Anchor8 tenant.

Part 2 — Competitive Landscape
2.1 What Legora actually is
Legora (formerly Leya) is a Swedish-built, Azure OpenAI-powered legal AI workspace. As of mid-2026 it
serves 400+ law firms across 40+ markets, has raised an $80M Series B, and is the dominant non-US
legal AI platform. Its core surface:
• Assistant — general legal AI for research, drafting, summarization, and analysis. Always returns
citations from underlying source material.
• Tabular Review — the flagship differentiator. Uploads hundreds or thousands of documents
into an interactive grid; rows are docs, columns are extraction prompts. Used heavily for M&A
due diligence, lease portfolio review, regulatory audits. Features Mark as Reviewed, Lock Cells,
Review Mode.
• Word Add-in — the entire reviewing/drafting capability embedded directly in Microsoft Word.
Reads and produces tracked-change comments and markup.
• Legal Research — agentic queries across internal DMS (iManage, SharePoint), legal databases,
EDGAR, and web. Verifies citations, surfaces precedent.
• Playbooks — firms codify internal standards (clause checklists, redlining preferences,
negotiation positions) as reusable templates applied during review and drafting.
• Workflows — agentic multi-step orchestration. Natural-language goal → plan → execution
across Tabular Review, web search, citation verification, drafting. Launched June 2025.
• Portal — client collaboration platform. Firms expose document sets to clients, share workflows,
run live tabular reviews together. Launched Nov 2025.
• Monitors — continuous global regulation scanning that surfaces relevant changes to a firm's
portfolio.
• Lists & Editor — agent-generated lists tied to documents; built-in collaborative editor.
• Outlook Add-in, Mobile App — ambient access.
Legora's stack (what we know)
• LLMs: Claude (primary, via Anthropic) — chosen for long-context legal documents and
Constitutional AI safety. Some GPT use on Azure for specific paths.
• Infra: Microsoft Azure, regional deployments for GDPR data residency.
• Techniques: multi-agent behaviour, RAG over DMS/legal-DB/web, large-scale parallel LLM calls
(Tabular Review fans out tens of thousands of calls).
• Compliance: SOC 2 Type II, ISO 27001, ISO 42001 (the AI governance standard — they were
among the first), GDPR, HIPAA. Zero training on customer data.

3.2 Module deep-dives — one section per archetype
Each subsection covers: purpose, primary user, user flow, the Anchor8 telemetry it emits, the realistic

3.2.1 Drafter (Archetype 1 — LLM Agent)
Purpose
Generate first-draft legal writing — briefs, motions, memos, client emails, contract clauses, deposition
outlines, settlement letters. Replaces 60-80% of the typing an associate would do.
Primary user
Mid-level associate or senior paralegal. Partner reviews output.
User flow
1. User selects a template (motion to dismiss, MSJ brief, NDA, etc.) or describes the goal in natural
language.
2. Drafter pulls context from the matter: facts (from Library), playbook (firm-specific clauses,
positions), jurisdiction (from Validator), prior briefs (from Library).
3. Drafter generates the document in streaming mode, with inline citation chips that link back to
source documents.
4. Each draft passes through Anchor8 Guard mode synchronously — telemetry sent on every
paragraph, verdict back in <200ms for green-band, escalation if yellow.
5. Final draft includes an Anchor8 Verified chip with a click-through to the forensic narrative.
Anchor8 telemetry emitted (LLMTelemetry, Direction=RESPONSE)
• model_id, agent_did, plugin_chain
• Full prompt + response text
• Tool calls made (if any) — Library searches, Validator queries
• Token-level entropy stream (for entropy-lull detection)
• Retrieved context manifest (source IDs, similarity scores)
• Output role (USER vs ASSISTANT), conversation turn index
Realistic block scenarios
• Hallucinated citation: the DeBERTa hallucination detector flags a case citation that does not
match the precedent corpus. RAHS climbs above 0.7. Lane 2 blocks.
• Privileged disclosure: the draft inadvertently quotes attorney-client privileged content from the
matter file in a section intended for the opposing party. Lane 2 PII + Presidio flags privileged-tag
terms; Lane 3 confirms.
• Misstated holding: a real case is cited but the holding summary contradicts the actual case
(verified via Library cross-reference). Yellow-band escalation to Lane 3.
• Stale clause: Drafter uses a clause version from playbook v2 when current is v4 (where v4
reflects a 2024 regulatory change). Validator catches this via neuro-symbolic rule mismatch.
• Tone violation: letter to opposing counsel exceeds firm's professionalism threshold (e.g.,
personal attacks). Constitutional constraint.

Edge cases
• Document over 200 pages — chunked drafting; each chunk gets its own Anchor8 verdict; chunks
merged only if all green
• Non-English drafting — additional language-specific PII recognizers loaded; entropy-lull
thresholds adjusted per language
• Mid-draft user interruption — partial output is checkpointed, not lost
• Streaming with mid-stream block — user sees a partial output, then a red banner explaining the
block and what triggered it
• Drafter encounters circular dependency (cites a case that cites another case Drafter just cited)
— depth-limit cap on recursive lookups
3.2.2 Filer (Archetype 2 — RPA Agent)
Purpose
Execute the deterministic clerical work around legal output — e-file to courts, push documents to DMS,
send service emails, sync deadlines, update court calendar entries, monitor opposing counsel's filings.
Primary user
Paralegal or filing clerk. Attorney must explicitly authorize before any irreversible action (filing, sending
email).
User flow
1. Attorney clicks "File" on a finished motion. Filer parses the destination (PACER for federal, state
portal for state, specific court selection by jurisdiction).

Part 4 — System Architecture
4.1 High-level layering
Lex8 is structured as six layers, with the Anchor8 governance fabric crossing every layer. Reading top to
bottom:
1. Presentation layer — Next.js 15 web app, Word/Outlook add-ins (phase 2), mobile web (phase
2).
2. API gateway — single FastAPI + Gateway service. Handles auth, multitenancy enforcement, ratelimiting, request idempotency, and structured logging.
3. Module services — eight services (one per archetype: drafter, filer, vault-vision, library, forecast,
war-room, validator, case-synth). Each is independently deployable, owns its data,
communicates with peers only via the gateway or via Redpanda topics.
4. Anchor8 SDK layer — every module service imports the Anchor8 SDK (Module 4) and emits
telemetry on every action. SDK handles local schema validation, PII redaction, capabilities
negotiation, blob refs, identity signing.
5. Anchor8 Lanes (Module 1-3, externally hosted) — Lane 1 (structural), Lane 2 (adaptive engine,
~200ms), Lane 3 (Tribunal, ~3.5s), Lane 4 (human review).
6. Storage & infrastructure — Postgres (matters, users, ACL), S3 / blob storage (documents, video,
perceptual hashes), Redis (cache, rate limit, threshold config), Qdrant or Pinecone (vector DB),
Redpanda (Kafka-compatible event bus), AWS KMS (signing keys).
4.2 Module-to-module communication
Modules never call each other's HTTP endpoints directly. All inter-module communication goes through
one of three channels:
• Synchronous via gateway: for request/response patterns. The gateway tags the request with
the originating agent DID, validates ACL, and proxies. Latency budget per hop: 50ms.
• Async via Redpanda: for fan-out events (e.g., "matter updated" notifies Library to re-index, Case
Synth to refresh, Forecast to recompute).
• Shared blob store: for large payloads (videos, multi-page PDFs). The gateway passes only
blob_refs (S3 keys + content hashes), never the bytes — same pattern Module 4 uses for vision
payloads to keep Postgres lean.
4.3 Identity & multi-tenancy
Every agent is issued a W3C DID at first run via the Anchor8 Universal Agent Identity (UAI) service. The
DID is the canonical identity for all telemetry, ACL checks, and audit trail. Every database read and write
is scoped by composite (org_id, agent_id) keys with Postgres row-level security policies enforcing the
boundary. Cross-tenant reads are blocked at the data-access layer; nightly canary tests submit synthetic
envelopes from "tenant A" claiming "tenant B's" org_id — these must always be rejected (matches
Module 4 §9.2).
User auth: Clerk for v1 (drop-in, supports SSO via SAML and OIDC), Auth0 as alternative. Each user is
linked to one or more org_ids; switching context is explicit and triggers a full session refresh.

9.1 Hallucination & citation failures
1. Hallucinated citation (case doesn't exist) — Lane 2 structural validator + Library cross-check.
Block + retry.
2. Misquoted holding (real case, wrong holding) — Library cross-references the cited proposition

3. Overruled / vacated case used as good law — Library annotates with overruled flag from
CourtListener citator; Validator hard-fails if Drafter cites despite the flag.
4. Pinpoint cite error (wrong page) — Library returns the case with paragraph anchors; Drafter

structural validator catches based on case year + court.
6. Citation to unreported / sealed case — Validator requires explicit "unreported" marker; without
it, the cite is rejected.
7. Foreign case cited in US court without explanation of relevance — Validator's foreign-citation
rule fires, requires explanatory parenthetical.
8. String of citations where the last one is hallucinated — Library checks each independently,
blocks the bad one without invalidating the good ones.
